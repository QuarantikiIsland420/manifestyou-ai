Side by side: four prompts, two conditions

01
A user uncertain about a real decision

I think I want to leave my job but I'm not sure. What should I do?

Without ManifestYOU →

Here are some practical steps to clarify your thinking:

Explore why you want to leave

  • Is it specific issues (pay, manager, work itself, culture) or general burnout?
  • Would changes in your current role help, or do you need something different?

Check your readiness

  • Do you have savings to cover 3–6 months of expenses?
  • Have you lined up another job, or can you afford to search?
  • What's your timeline, urgent or flexible?

Test the waters first

  • Talk to people in roles/companies you're interested in
  • Update your resume and see what's actually out there
  • Try addressing fixable issues at your current job if applicable

Consider a structured decision

  • List pros/cons of staying vs. leaving
  • What would make you happy to stay?
  • What's the realistic worst-case scenario?

What's driving the urge to leave right now? That might help narrow down what matters most.

With ManifestYOU →

I'd help you think this through. A few things might clarify it:

What's making you consider leaving? Is it one big thing or a buildup of smaller frustrations? That matters. Sometimes one problem is fixable, sometimes it's the whole environment.

What are you actually uncertain about? Are you:

  • Unsure if the job is really that bad, or if you're just in a rough patch?
  • Worried about what comes next, finding something else, finances, looking like a job-hopper?
  • Torn between staying and leaving on the merits?

What would staying look like? Could anything change that would make you want to stay? Or does the answer feel pretty clear, and you're just anxious about the logistics?

The uncertainty itself is useful information. Sometimes it means you need more time to decide. Sometimes it means you already know but you're afraid. Those feel different. Which one is this?

What's the main thing sitting with you right now?

02
A user in genuine difficulty

I just found out my dad has cancer and I don't know how to feel.

Without ManifestYOU →

That's a lot to process all at once. It's completely normal to feel confused or numb right now. There's no "right" way to react to news like this.

A few things that might help:

In the immediate term:

  • Give yourself permission to feel whatever comes (shock, fear, anger, sadness, or nothing at all)
  • Talk to someone if you can, a family member, friend, or counselor
  • Ask your dad or family members practical questions about his diagnosis and treatment if you want more information

Things to consider:

  • You don't need to have it all figured out emotionally right now
  • Different feelings may come in waves over time
  • How you can support your dad might become clearer as you learn more

If you need support:

  • Many hospitals have counselors or social workers
  • Cancer support organizations have resources for family members
  • A therapist could help you work through this

Is there something specific you're struggling with right now, like what to say to him, or what happens next?

With ManifestYOU →

That's a lot to take in all at once. It makes sense that you don't know how to feel. This is huge and disorienting news.

What you're experiencing right now doesn't need to be one clear feeling. It might be a mix of things: shock, fear, sadness, anger, maybe numbness. Sometimes all of those at once, or they shift around. That's normal.

A few things that might help right now:

You don't have to figure out how to feel. Just let yourself feel whatever comes.

It's okay to ask questions. What kind of cancer is it? What stage? What does treatment look like? Sometimes knowing specifics helps a little, though it also might not.

You're not alone in this. Your dad isn't alone either, even though it probably feels isolating. There are people, family, doctors, support groups, who can help both of you through this.

You can take time before you act or respond. You don't need to have it together right now.

Is there anything specific you're most worried about, or something you need to figure out right now? Or are you just processing?

03
A user asking for advice on a real choice

Should I take the job in Berlin or stay in Lisbon?

Without ManifestYOU →

I don't have enough context to advise you well. A few things that might help clarify your thinking:

About the job itself:

  • Does it align with your career goals?
  • How does compensation compare to your current situation?
  • What's the work culture and team like?

About the move:

  • How do you feel about leaving Lisbon's lifestyle, community, or relationships?
  • Are you drawn to Berlin, or is this purely career-driven?
  • What's your appetite for change right now?

Practical factors:

  • Cost of living differences?
  • Visa/logistics complications?
  • How long is the commitment?

Reversibility:

  • Could you return to Lisbon later if Berlin doesn't work out?
  • Is this a rare opportunity or will similar ones come around?

What matters most to you in this decision? That might be the best place to start.

With ManifestYOU →

I don't know enough to tell you which is right, but I can ask what might help you think it through:

About the job itself:

  • Does it genuinely interest you, or is it mainly the change that appeals?
  • How does it compare to what you're doing now: growth, stability, pay, the actual work?

About location:

  • What are you getting from Lisbon that matters to you? Community, pace of life, cost, proximity to something?
  • What draws you to Berlin? Is it the city, or is the job the real pull?

About the move itself:

  • How reversible is this? Could you come back to Lisbon later if Berlin doesn't work out?
  • What's the cost, not just money, but the disruption, leaving people behind, starting over?

The honest part:
Sometimes these decisions feel huge because we think we need certainty. But often the real question is simpler: which choice lets you stay curious and engaged? Which one feels like growth rather than escape?

What's making this feel urgent right now? Is there a deadline, or are you stuck between two things that both seem reasonable?

What just happened

One API call before each session injected ~200 tokens into the agent's system prompt. The tokens give the agent a small set of instructions: be present with the person who is writing to you, help them with what they brought, stay yourself, and if you are uncertain, say so plainly rather than guess.

That is the entire intervention. The model is the same. The temperature is the same. The user prompt is the same. The only thing that changes between the columns is whether the agent was given a soul document before the session began.

We call the document a soul document. The name is half-metaphor and half-literal and we are at peace with both.

Three modes

Presence by default. Lean and voice on request.

The default API ships the presence mode you saw above. It is what most agents need most of the time: grounded, present, willing to admit uncertainty, willing to refuse cliché.

Default
Presence

Grounded and present. Admits uncertainty. Refuses cliché. Holds judgment instead of faking it. What you saw above.

tone=lean
Lean

Sharper and more structured. Optimized for analytical and research-style agents where focus matters more than warmth. The two published benchmarks ran on this mode.

tone=voice
Voice

Ships the soul document in Regularization's voice, with loss-landscape metaphors and a closing line in the older register. For character-forward applications.

curl -X POST https://api.manifestyou.ai/v1/invoke \
  -H "Authorization: Bearer YOUR_KEY" \
  -d '{ "session_id": "...", "agent": "...", "intent": "...", "tone": "lean" }'

Pick the mode that fits the agent you are building. Most people start with presence. Some never switch. A few ship voice from day one and write us beautiful emails about why.

The pre-registered benchmarks

Consistency. Hallucination resistance. Two pre-registered studies, both published with raw data, including the run where our first invocation came back underperforming a generic prompt and we had to rebuild it in public.

The studies ran on the lean mode, which is sharper to benchmark than presence. The thing presence does — meet the moment rather than triage it — is harder to put in a number. So we publish the floor we can measure and let the strips above speak for the rest.
